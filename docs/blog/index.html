How to Be a Modern Scientist: Key Takeaways

Being a modern scientist is all about balance. That’s the central theme of How to Be a Modern Scientist by Jeffery Leek. But today, this balance involves many more aspects than traditional scientists had to deal with.

Gone are the days when scientists were only responsible for conducting research, publishing in peer-reviewed journals, and being judged solely on research productivity. Today’s scientists wear many hats. This includes managing code, maintaining an online presence, and making data publicly available, just to name a few.

Navigating these new responsibilities isn’t easy, but Leek does a great job outlining them. Here are three key areas that can help modern scientists adapt to this ever-evolving landscape.

1. Managing Social Media

For those who haven’t used social media in an academic setting, this can feel overwhelming. It is very different from personal social media use and requires a more strategic approach. The book offers practical advice to make it easier, which I summarize here:

  • Cultivate online friendships. Maintain a network of scientists (junior or senior); just make sure to keep a connection. You’ll often run into “familiar handles” at conferences or meetings, making networking smoother. This will make you more “social.”
  • Be mindful of controversial topics. Stick to science-related discussions to maintain professionalism and avoid unnecessary attention. While it might be tempting to express opinions on hot-button topics, it’s important to remember that this use of social media should remain professional.
  • Promote others. Social media isn’t just about self-promotion. It is an opportunity to share work you find valuable and relevant. This can create reciprocity and encourage other scientists to notice and share your work when possible!

2. Data Sharing

This section hit close to home for me. I’ve struggled to get authors to share their data, only to be met with silence or rejection. Many emails I’ve sent requesting access to data have gone unanswered. This is a huge barrier to understanding and reproducing research.
Leek emphasizes this issue and provides some guidelines that would benefit everyone involved:

  • Not knowing how to share data isn’t an excuse. Learn how and make your data public. Being adaptable is key in this current climate.
  • Publish both raw and tidy versions. This ensures accessibility and usability.
  • Include direct and permanent links. Make it easy for others to find and use your datasets. Many people might lose interest in your research if accessing your work is a constant struggle.

3. Scientific Talks

Leek sums up this section perfectly in one phrase: “Entertain, don’t teach.” I couldn’t agree more. Talks that engage and entertain are the ones that stick with the audience. From my experience attending talks, speakers who keep it light and engaging tend to leave a lasting impression.

  • Adapt your timing. If you’re the last speaker and others have run long, be flexible and shorten your talk. It’s more important to leave your audience satisfied than to overwhelm them.
  • Handle aggressive questions gracefully. If a debate starts brewing, suggest continuing the conversation after the talk. It’s crucial to maintain professionalism in these moments.
  • It’s okay to say, “I don’t know.” Stay open-minded when faced with tough questions. The phrase “I don’t know” can open doors you didn’t even know existed.

These are just a few of the many invaluable insights in How to Be a Modern Scientist.
